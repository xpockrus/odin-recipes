Basic Lefse: Norwegian Potato Flatbread

Description

Even the Valkyries get hungry around snack time! What better way to bring the family together than with some Lefse?

A Norwegian Lefse is prepared on a specialized griddle, but fret not; you can get by with whatever you have at home.
Think of Lefse as a heartier crepe, chewier tortilla, and a thinner, grilled Naan.
It is important to let the dough rest for at least 8 hours, so it's highly recommended to begin this process the night before.

Ingredients

  • 2 pounds Idaho russett potatoes (about 5 potatoes)
  • 1/4 cup heavy cream
  • 1 tbsp unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 tbsp sugar
  • 1 tsp sea salt
  • 1 cup flour (plus more for dusting)
    For Serving:
  • Butter, to taste
  • Sugar, to taste

Steps

Make the Dough

  1. Gather the ingredients
  2. Preheat the oven to 80°C (175°F)
  3. Peel the potatoes thoroughly and coarsely chop them into 1-inch pieces
  4. In a stockpot, bring water to a boil and add the potatoes. Boil them until they are fork-tender, about 15 to 20 minutes.
  5. Drain the boiled potatoes well. Place them on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper and bring them into the hot oven for 15 minutes to dry them further.
  6. Remove the potatoes from the oven and pass them through a ricer. You need them to be finely riced, so twice through the ricer might give you a better texture. You should have at least 4 cups of riced potatoes.
  7. Mix in the heavy cream, butter, sugar, and salt.
  8. Mix well and place the potato dough in the refrigerator, covered, to chill overnight.

Shape the Lefse

  1. Preheat a lefse griddle or your skillet of choice to 425 F. You need a very hot surface to properly cook the lefse. Use a pastry blender to cut the flour into the chilled potatoes, or vigorously knead the flour in until you have a smooth dough.
  2. With the help of an ice cream scoop, divide the dough into 16 to 20 biscuit-sized balls.
  3. Generously flour a pastry cloth or board and a rolling pin. Roll out each piece of dough into a 12-inch circle, dusting with more flour as needed.
  4. Carefully lift the circle with a lefse stick or the handle of a flat wooden spatula. Transfer it quickly to the griddle.
  5. Cook the lefse on the griddle until brown spots begin to appear.
  6. Flip and cook the other side.
  7. Remove the cooked lefse to a plate lined with a damp clean cloth to cool. Cover with another damp cloth. Continue to cook the remaining dough balls until you've used all of the dough.
  8. Serve the lefse smeared with butter to taste and a sprinkle or two of sugar.
  9. Skol! You have nothing left to do but enjoy your hard work. An effort worthy of Asgard
